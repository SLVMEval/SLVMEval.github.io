Abstract

We introduce SLVMEval, a benchmark for meta-evaluating text-to-video (T2V) evaluation systems. SLVMEval focuses on assessing these systems on long videos of up to 10,486 seconds (approximately 3 hours). Our benchmark targets a fundamental requirement: whether systems can accurately judge video quality in settings that are easy for humans to assess. We adopt a pairwise comparison-based meta-evaluation framework. Building on dense video captioning datasets, we synthetically degrade source videos to create controlled "high-quality vs. low-quality" pairs across 10 distinct aspects. We then use crowdsourcing to filter and retain only those pairs in which the degradation is clearly perceptible, thereby establishing the final testbed. Using this testbed, we assess the reliability of existing evaluation systems in ranking these pairs. Our experiments show that human evaluators identify the better long video with 84.7%–96.8% accuracy, while in 9 of the 10 aspects, the accuracy of these systems falls short of human judgment, revealing weaknesses in text-to-long video evaluation.

Long-Video Focus

First meta-evaluation benchmark designed specifically for long videos, covering durations up to 10,486 seconds — far beyond existing benchmarks that focus on short clips.

Synthetic Degradation Pipeline

We synthetically degrade source videos along 10 fine-grained aspects to create controlled high-quality vs. low-quality pairs, validated by crowdsourced human annotation.

Revealing the Gap

State-of-the-art automatic evaluation systems (including GPT-5) lag behind human performance on 9 out of 10 aspects, and their accuracy degrades as video duration increases.

Benchmark Overview

Figure 1. Overview of SLVMEval pipeline

Long videos → Aspect-specific synthetic degradation → Human verification → SLVMEval pairs → Meta-evaluation of automatic systems

Figure 1. Overview of SLVMEval. We construct human-validated pairs of original and aspect-specifically degraded long videos, and test various automatic evaluation systems. Although human evaluators reliably pick the better video, all current automatic evaluation systems still lag behind human performance on most aspects, revealing critical weaknesses in T2V evaluation.

SLVMEval is designed to probe whether evaluation systems possess the minimum capability required for T2V model development. We target long videos — spanning several minutes to nearly three hours — which are precisely the regimes where current automatic systems struggle most. By constructing paired videos that differ in exactly one specified quality dimension, we enable precise, fine-grained meta-evaluation.

Evaluation Aspects

SLVMEval covers 10 evaluation aspects organized into two categories. For each aspect, we construct paired videos by applying a controlled synthetic degradation to the original, keeping all other factors unchanged.

Figure 2. SLVMEval viewpoints and aspect-specific degrading operations

Figure 2. SLVMEval organizes 10 aspects into Video Quality and Video-Text Consistency groups, each paired with a specific degrading operation. Lower panels show example pairs — paired videos enable precise meta-evaluation of whether an automatic evaluator can reliably identify the high-quality video.
Video Quality (5 aspects)
1
Aesthetics
Reduce the contrast of selected clips to assess frame-level aesthetic quality.
2
Technical Quality
Downscale the resolution to detect low-resolution artifacts.
3
Appearance Style
Apply mismatched artistic style transfer (e.g., oil painting, manga, sketch) to selected clips.
4
Background Consistency
Replace original backgrounds with random landscape images to break temporal visual stability.
5
Object Integrity
Erase prompt-specified objects via inpainting to remove key scene elements.
Video-Text Consistency (5 aspects)
6
Color
Edit colors of prompt-specified objects to different colors, breaking color consistency with the description.
7
Dynamics Degree
Replace motion-containing clips with static middle frames to reduce dynamics described in the prompt.
8
Comprehensiveness
Remove several clips from the original video, reducing coverage of all prompt-described events.
9
Spatial Relationship
Horizontally flip clips mentioning left/right relations, inverting spatial consistency.
10
Temporal Flow
Relocate several consecutive clips to random positions, breaking the described event order.

Key Findings

For most aspects and systems, accuracy decreases as video duration increases (negative Spearman ρS).

Automatic Systems Fall Short

Duration Sensitivity

Human Baseline

Human evaluators achieve 84.7%–96.8% accuracy across all 10 aspects, establishing a strong human-level target for future T2V evaluation systems to reach.
